Charles Booth

Charles Booth (1840–1916) was a British social reformer and philanthropist best known for his pioneering research on poverty and social conditions in late Victorian London. Concerned by debates over the extent of poverty in Britain, Booth undertook a monumental study to document the lives of the working class. Between 1886 and 1903, he published the influential Life and Labour of the People in London, a multi-volume work that provided an in-depth analysis of economic and social conditions across the city. Booth’s research introduced the concept of a "poverty line," a threshold that defined the minimum income required for basic subsistence, and revealed that approximately 30% of London’s population lived in poverty—far more than was commonly believed at the time.

Booth's work was groundbreaking in its use of data and detailed mapping. He created "poverty maps" that categorized streets and neighborhoods by color to reflect the varying degrees of wealth and deprivation. These maps not only highlighted the stark inequalities in London but also inspired future studies in sociology and urban planning. Booth's findings challenged prevailing attitudes about poverty, showing that it was often a result of systemic issues rather than individual failings. His research influenced social reforms in Britain, including the introduction of old-age pensions and debates about state intervention to address poverty. Charles Booth is remembered as a trailblazer in social science, whose work laid the foundation for modern approaches to understanding and addressing urban poverty.
