Luddite

The Luddites were a group of skilled workers in early 19th-century England who protested against the introduction of new machinery during the Industrial Revolution. Named after their mythical leader, “Ned Ludd,” the Luddites emerged between 1811 and 1816 in textile-producing regions such as Nottinghamshire, Yorkshire, and Lancashire. They were primarily concerned with the mechanization of textile production, particularly the use of stocking frames, power looms, and spinning frames, which threatened their livelihoods. The new machines allowed manufacturers to produce goods faster and more cheaply, often using unskilled laborers, which led to job losses for skilled artisans. In response, the Luddites resorted to direct action, smashing machines and attacking factories in an attempt to halt industrial progress and protect their way of life.

Although the Luddites were initially supported by many local communities, their actions faced harsh government crackdowns. The British government, fearing wider unrest, deployed military forces to suppress the movement and passed laws, such as the Frame Breaking Act, which made machine-breaking a capital offense. Many Luddites were arrested, executed, or transported to penal colonies. Over time, the movement waned as industrialization continued to advance, but the Luddites remain a symbol of resistance to technological change and its potential to disrupt traditional ways of life. Today, the term “Luddite” is often used to describe someone who resists or distrusts new technology, though the original Luddites were more focused on protecting their economic survival rather than opposing technology itself.
