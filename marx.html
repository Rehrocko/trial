Karl Marx

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, sociologist, and revolutionary thinker best known for his critique of capitalism and his influential role in shaping socialist and communist ideologies. Alongside Friedrich Engels, he authored The Communist Manifesto (1848), a political pamphlet advocating for the overthrow of capitalist systems and the establishment of a classless society. Marx’s most comprehensive work, Das Kapital, provided a detailed analysis of capitalist economies, focusing on concepts such as surplus value, exploitation, and the alienation of labor. He argued that capitalism inherently produced class conflict between the bourgeoisie (owners of production) and the proletariat (working class), predicting that this conflict would eventually lead to revolution and the establishment of a socialist system.

Marx’s ideas, often referred to as Marxism, profoundly influenced political movements and economic theories worldwide. While his vision of a proletarian revolution and a classless society has been subject to various interpretations, his critiques of inequality and the concentration of wealth remain relevant in discussions about economic systems. Marx’s theories laid the foundation for many socialist and communist movements in the 19th and 20th centuries, including the Russian Revolution of 1917. However, his legacy is complex; while some view him as a champion of workers' rights and social justice, others criticize the authoritarian regimes that later claimed to follow his ideas. Regardless of perspective, Marx remains one of the most influential and debated figures in modern history.
